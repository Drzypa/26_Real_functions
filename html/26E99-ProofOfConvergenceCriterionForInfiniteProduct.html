proof of convergence criterion for infinite product


Consider the partial product Pn=∏i=1npi.

By definition we say that the infinite product ∏n=1∞pn is convergent iff Pn is convergent.

Suppose every pn>0

ln is a continuous bijection from ℝ+ to ℝ, therefore limn→∞⁡an=a⇔limn→∞⁡ln⁡(an)=ln⁡(a), provided an>0 and a>0.

so saying Pn→P>0 is equivalent to saying that ln⁡(Pn) converges.

Since ln⁡(Pn)=ln⁡(∏i=1npi)=∑i=1nln⁡(pi), the infinite product converges to a positive value iff the series ∑n=1∞ln⁡(pn) is convergent.

In particular, if the infinite product converges to a positive value, then ln⁡(pn)→0⟹pn→1.

Pn→0, is equivalent to saying ∑n=1∞ln⁡(pn)=-∞

For the second part of the theorem:

∏n=1∞(1+pn) converges absolutely to a positive value iff ∑n=1∞pn converges absolutely.

as we have seen, 1+pn→1⟹pn→0

consider: limx→0⁡ln⁡(1+x)x=1 (this is easy to prove since by Taylor’s expansion ln⁡(1+x)=x+O⁢(x2))

Since pn→0 we can say that limn→∞⁡ln⁡(1+pn)pn=1 and by the limit comparison test, either both ∑n=1∞ln⁡(1+pn) and ∑i=1npi converge or diverge.
