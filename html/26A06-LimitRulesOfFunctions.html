limit rules of functions


Theorem 1.

Let f and g be two real (http://planetmath.org/RealFunction) or complex functions.  Suppose that there exist the limits  limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)  and  limx→x0⁡g⁢(x).  Then there exist the limits  limx→x0⁡[f⁢(x)±g⁢(x)],  limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x)  and, if  limx→x0⁡g⁢(x)≠0,  also  limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)/g⁢(x), and

  1. 1.

    limx→x0⁡[f⁢(x)±g⁢(x)]=limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)±limx→x0⁡g⁢(x),

  2. 2.

    limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x)=limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)⋅limx→x0⁡g⁢(x),

  3. 3.

    limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)g⁢(x)=limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)limx→x0⁡g⁢(x),

  4. 4.

    limx→x0⁡c=c where⁢c⁢is⁢a⁢constant.

These rules are used in limit calculations and in proving the corresponding differentiation rules (sum rule, product rule etc.).

In 1, the domains of f and g could be any topological space (not necessarily ℝ or ℂ).

There are limit rules of sequences (http://planetmath.org/Sequence).

As well, one often needs the

Theorem 2.

If there exists the limit  limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)=a  and if g is continuous at the point  x=a, then there exists the limit  limx→x0⁡g⁢(f⁢(x)), and

limx→x0⁡g⁢(f⁢(x))=g⁢(limx→x0⁡f⁢(x)).
