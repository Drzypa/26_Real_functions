fundamental theorem of calculus


Let f:[a,b]→𝐑 be a continuous function, let c∈[a,b] be given and consider the integral function F defined on [a,b] as

F⁢(x)=∫cxf⁢(t)⁢𝑑t.

Then F is an antiderivative of f that is, F is differentiable in [a,b] and

F′⁢(x)=f⁢(x)  ∀x∈[a,b].

The previous relation rewritten as

dd⁢x⁢∫cxf⁢(t)⁢𝑑t=f⁢(x)

shows that the differentiation operator dd⁢x is the inverse of the integration operator ∫cx. This formula is sometimes called Newton-Leibniz formula.

On the other hand if f:[a,b]→𝐑 is a continuous function and G:[a,b]→𝐑 is any antiderivative of f, i.e. G′⁢(x)=f⁢(x) for all x∈[a,b], then

∫abf⁢(t)⁢𝑑t=G⁢(b)-G⁢(a). (1)

This shows that up to a constant, the integration operator is the inverse of the derivative operator:

∫axD⁢G=G-G⁢(a).

Notes

Equation (1) is sometimes called “Barrow’s rule” or “Barrow’s formula”.
