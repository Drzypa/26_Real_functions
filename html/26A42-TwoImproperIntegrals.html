two improper integrals


Let us consider first the improper integral

I⁢(k):=∫0∞1-cos⁡k⁢xx2⁢𝑑x.

The derivative I′⁢(k) may be formed by differentiating under the integral sign (http://planetmath.org/DifferentiationUnderIntegralSign):

I′⁢(k)=∫0∞(∂∂⁡k⁢1-cos⁡k⁢xx2)⁢𝑑x=∫0∞sin⁡k⁢xx⁢𝑑x=∫0∞sin⁡tt⁢𝑑t

Here, the last form has been gotten by the substitution (http://planetmath.org/ChangeOfVariableInDefiniteIntegral)  k⁢x=t.  But since by the parent entry (http://planetmath.org/SineIntegralInInfinity) we have

∫0∞sin⁡tt⁢𝑑t=π2

and since  I⁢(0)=0, we can write

I⁢(k)=∫0kπ2⁢𝑑k=π⁢k2.

Thus we have evaluated the integral I⁢(k):

∫0∞1-cos⁡k⁢xx2⁢𝑑x=π⁢k2. (1)

The formula (1) gives

I⁢(1)=∫0∞1-cos⁡xx2⁢𝑑x=π2.

We use here the consequence formula

1-cos⁡x= 2⁢sin2⁡x2

of the double angle formula  cos⁡2⁢α=1-2⁢sin2⁡α,  obtaining

π2= 2⁢∫0∞sin2⁡x2x2⁢𝑑x=∫0∞sin2⁡uu2⁢𝑑u,

where the substitution  x2=u  has produced the last form.  Accordingly, we can write as result the formula

∫0∞(sin⁡xx)2⁢𝑑x=π2. (2)
